Noise Prediction Error

A step-by-step derivation of how variational inference on a Gaussian diffusion process collapses into a simple MSE objective.

01 / 04

The Intractable Problem

Given data \(x_0\), a diffusion model defines a forward process that gradually corrupts it into noise, and a reverse process that attempts to undo the corruption step by step.

The forward process is a fixed Markov chain:

Forward Process
\[ q(x_{1:T} \mid x_0) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} q(x_t \mid x_{t-1}), \quad q(x_t \mid x_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(x_t;\, \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\, x_{t-1},\, \beta_t I) \]

The reverse process is what we want to learn:

Reverse Process (learned)
\[ p_\theta(x_{0:T}) = p(x_T) \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_\theta(x_{t-1} \mid x_t) \]
⚠️

The true log-likelihood \(\log p_\theta(x_0)\) requires marginalizing over all latent trajectories \(x_{1:T}\) — a sum over an exponentially large space. This is completely intractable.

02 / 04

Variational Inference & the ELBO

Variational inference sidesteps intractability by introducing a tractable distribution \(q\) to approximate the true posterior, then optimizing a lower bound instead.

The ELBO (Evidence Lower Bound) follows from Jensen's inequality:

ELBO
\[ \log p_\theta(x_0) \;\geq\; \mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\log \frac{p_\theta(x_{0:T})}{q(x_{1:T} \mid x_0)}\right] = \mathcal{L}_\text{ELBO} \]

The gap between the bound and the true likelihood is exactly the KL divergence:

KL Gap
\[ \log p_\theta(x_0) = \mathcal{L}_\text{ELBO} + D_{KL}\!\left(q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0) \,\|\, p_\theta(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)\right) \]

After algebraic manipulation, the ELBO decomposes into interpretable per-timestep terms:

ELBO Decomposition (DDPM)
\[ \mathcal{L} = \underbrace{D_{KL}(q(x_T\mid x_0) \| p(x_T))}_{\mathcal{L}_T \text{ — prior matching}} \;+\; \sum_{t=2}^{T} \underbrace{D_{KL}(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t, x_0) \| p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t))}_{\mathcal{L}_{t-1} \text{ — denoising}} \;-\; \underbrace{\log p_\theta(x_0\mid x_1)}_{\mathcal{L}_0 \text{ — reconstruction}} \]

\(\mathcal{L}_T\) is constant (no parameters). The dominant terms are \(\mathcal{L}_{t-1}\): train \(p_\theta\) to match the true reverse step \(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t, x_0)\).

03 / 04

From KL Divergence to MSE

Here is where the loss function crystallizes. Each step follows necessarily from the previous.

Both distributions are Gaussian

The forward posterior \(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t, x_0)\) is tractable and Gaussian — this is the key property of the Markov chain. The model \(p_\theta\) is also parameterized as Gaussian:

\[ q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t, x_0) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\tilde{\mu}_t,\; \tilde{\beta}_t I\right), \qquad p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu_\theta(x_t, t),\; \sigma_t^2 I\right) \]

KL of two Gaussians = squared mean difference

When two Gaussians share the same variance, their KL reduces to:

\[ D_{KL}\!\left(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \sigma^2) \,\|\, \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \sigma^2)\right) = \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mu_1 - \mu_2\|^2 \]

So the denoising objective becomes: minimize the squared distance between the predicted mean and the true mean.

\[ \mathcal{L}_{t-1} \;\propto\; \|\tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0) - \mu_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2 \]

Reparametrize \(x_0\) in terms of noise

The forward process satisfies a closed-form marginal (using \(\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^t \alpha_s\)):

\[ x_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\;\epsilon, \qquad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \]

Solving for \(x_0\):

\[ x_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}}\!\left(x_t - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\;\epsilon\right) \]

Substituting into \(\tilde{\mu}_t\) and simplifying:

\[ \tilde{\mu}_t = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\!\left(x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}}\;\epsilon\right) \]

Matching means ≡ predicting the noise

Parametrize the model's mean to have the same form:

\[ \mu_\theta(x_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\!\left(x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}}\;\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\right) \]

Substituting into the mean-matching objective, all shared terms cancel:

\[ \|\tilde{\mu}_t - \mu_\theta\|^2 \;\propto\; \|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2 \]
04 / 04

The Final Loss

Ho et al. (DDPM, 2020) further simplify by dropping the time-dependent weighting across steps, treating all timesteps uniformly. This "simple" loss works better in practice:

DDPM Simple Loss — The Training Objective
\[ \boxed{\mathcal{L}_\text{simple} = \mathbb{E}_{t,\, x_0,\, \epsilon}\!\left[\,\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2\,\right]} \]

This tells us: sample a random timestep \(t\), add the corresponding noise \(\epsilon\) to \(x_0\) to get \(x_t\), and train the network to predict what noise was added.

Key Insight

The noise prediction MSE is not an arbitrary or heuristic choice — it is the exact algebraic consequence of applying variational inference to a Gaussian Markov diffusion chain, then reparametrizing the mean prediction in terms of the added noise. Every step is forced by the math.
