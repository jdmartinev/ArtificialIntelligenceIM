1) VAE pipeline: encoder → latent → decoder

The encoder produces parameters of \(q(z|x)\). We sample using reparameterization and decode with \(p(x|z)\).

\[ \mathcal{L}(x)= -\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q(z|x)}[\log p(x|z)]}_{\text{reconstruction}} + \underbrace{KL(q(z|x)\|p(z))}_{\text{regularization}}. \]

2) ELBO gap: why it is a lower bound

The identity that matters:

\[ \log p(x)=\text{ELBO}(x)+KL\big(q(z|x)\|p(z|x)\big). \]
Because \(KL(\cdot\|\cdot)\ge 0\), we always have \(\text{ELBO}(x)\le \log p(x)\). Maximizing ELBO pushes \(q(z|x)\) closer to the true posterior \(p(z|x)\).

Reading: as training improves, ELBO increases and the gap (a KL) tends to shrink.

3) What KL regularization does (latent-space intuition)

Most VAEs choose a simple prior, often:

\[ p(z)=\mathcal{N}(0,I). \]

Then \(KL(q(z|x)\|p(z))\) discourages the encoder from putting posteriors in “weird” places, helping the latent space remain continuous and sampleable.

Intuition: KL makes the collection of per-datapoint Gaussians \(q(z|x)\) overlap and stay near the prior, so sampling \(z\sim p(z)\) yields meaningful decoded samples.
\[ \text{Sampling works: } z\sim p(z)\Rightarrow x\sim p(x|z) \quad \text{only if the learned latents match the prior reasonably well.} \]

4) Teaching script (short)

1) We want log p(x) but can’t compute it.
2) Introduce q(z|x) and derive ELBO via Jensen.
3) ELBO = recon - KL(q||p).
4) Sampling blocks gradients → reparam: z = μ + σ ε.
5) KL shapes latent space so sampling from p(z) produces meaningful x.
